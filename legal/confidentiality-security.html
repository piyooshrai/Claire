ABA Model Rule 1.6: Confidentiality Framework

Rule 1.6 protects "information relating to the representation"—not just formal attorney-client communications, but all client-related information learned during representation. This includes facts shared in confidence, legal strategies, settlement positions, work product, fee arrangements, client identity (in some circumstances), and even the fact that an attorney-client relationship exists.

Comment [18] addresses technology directly: "A lawyer must act competently to safeguard information relating to the representation of a client against inadvertent or unauthorized disclosure by the lawyer or other persons who are participating in the representation." This comment was amended in 2012 specifically to address cybersecurity and third-party vendor risks.

The key phrase: "persons who are participating in the representation or who are subject to the lawyer's supervision." This includes IT vendors, document review services, e-discovery providers—and AI assistants. The obligation is clear: Using AI tools requires understanding how those tools handle client data.

What Rule 1.6 Requires for AI Vendors

When you engage any third-party vendor (including AI tools), you must:

  1. Understand the technology: How does the AI system work? Does it store client data? Does it use client data for training? How long is data retained?
  2. Verify security measures: Is data encrypted in transit and at rest? What access controls exist? Are there audit logs? Has the vendor undergone independent security audits?
  3. Review contractual protections: Does the vendor agreement include confidentiality obligations, data ownership provisions, and prohibition on using client data for other purposes?
  4. Monitor ongoing compliance: Periodically review the vendor's security practices and update agreements as technology changes.

Work Product Doctrine & AI-Generated Documents

Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 26(b)(3) protects documents "prepared in anticipation of litigation or for trial" from discovery. This work product doctrine shields legal strategy, mental impressions, and case analysis from opposing counsel.

AI-generated documents raise novel questions that courts are beginning to address. In In re Keurig Green Mountain Single-Serve Coffee Antitrust Litig., No. 14-md-2542 (S.D.N.Y. 2016), a court rejected arguments that AI-powered document review waived work product protection, holding that "the deliberative process of attorneys using technology to identify responsive documents is protected."

How Work Product Protection Applies to My Output

I generate documents based on your firm's templates and your specific instructions. The output is attorney work product because it reflects:

  • Legal strategy: You decide what arguments to make, what cases to cite, what facts to emphasize. I execute your strategic choices.
  • Case analysis: When I analyze documents or identify issues, I'm performing work under attorney supervision—the same as a paralegal.
  • Advocacy decisions: You choose tone, emphasis, and persuasive approach. I implement those choices.

The fact that I assisted with drafting doesn't waive privilege any more than having a paralegal draft a document would. The key is attorney supervision and control—you remain responsible for the work product.

Ethical Duties & Compliance

ABA Model Rule 1.1 requires competent representation, including "keep[ing] abreast of changes in the law and its practice, including the benefits and risks associated with relevant technology." Using AI tools competently means understanding how the system works, reviewing output for accuracy, supervising the work, and ensuring confidentiality compliance.

State Bar Guidance

Several state bars have issued ethics opinions on AI use:

California (2023-204): Lawyers may use AI tools if they maintain competence, supervise output, protect confidences through vendor agreements, and avoid conflicts of interest.

New York (2024-01): Disclosure to clients is required if AI tools access confidential information or generate work product without substantial attorney review. Disclosure in engagement letter satisfies this requirement.

Florida (23-2): Lawyers using AI must verify accuracy of output, understand limitations, protect confidentiality through vendor contracts, and bill clients fairly.

I'm designed to satisfy all state bar requirements through transparent architecture, comprehensive security, zero data retention, and complete audit trails.

Deep Dive: Ephemeral Data Orchestration vs Traditional Cloud Storage

Understanding the technical distinction between ephemeral access and persistent storage is essential for evaluating AI privilege risk. This deep technical analysis explains why orchestration architecture fundamentally eliminates privilege vulnerabilities that plague traditional legal tech.

Section 1: The Privilege Problem with Traditional Legal Tech

Traditional legal technology vendors operate on centralized data lake architectures. When a client submits information, that data is typically stored in the vendor's database for 30-90 days minimum (sometimes indefinitely), used to train machine learning models for product improvement, accessible to vendor's staff for "quality assurance" and technical troubleshooting, and subject to vendor's data breach risks, subpoenas, and government requests.

In United States v. Kovel, 296 F.2d 918 (2d Cir. 1961), courts established that privilege extends to third parties assisting attorneys—but only when their involvement is necessary for legal advice and confidentiality is maintained. If the third party has independent business purposes for accessing the data (like training AI models), privilege protection weakens significantly.

Recent cases have scrutinized technology vendors' access to privileged information. In discovery disputes, judges have found privilege waived when law firms used technology platforms that retained copies of privileged documents or allowed vendor staff access "for system administration." This creates substantial privilege risk for traditional cloud-based legal tech.

Section 2: Ephemeral Architecture Eliminates Privilege Risk

Claire operates on fundamentally different architecture: ephemeral data orchestration rather than centralized storage. The distinction:

Traditional Architecture: Data copied from your system into vendor's database → stored indefinitely → accessed by vendor staff → potentially used for training → accessible to subpoenas

Ephemeral Architecture: Your data remains in your system → I access via API for specific task → process in temporary session memory → generate output → session memory discarded → zero retention

The data never leaves your control. This architectural distinction is legally significant. Attorney-client privilege is most secure when no third party possesses the privileged information. By operating as an ephemeral processor rather than a data repository, I maintain the confidentiality necessary for privilege protection.

Section 3: Technical Implementation Details

API-Based Access: I integrate with your practice management system via secure APIs (Clio REST API, MyCase APIs, PracticePanther APIs). Instead of copying data, I request specific information for specific tasks.

Session-Scoped Access Tokens: Each task generates a temporary access token valid only for that specific session. Token expires immediately after task completion, preventing re-access to data without explicit new authorization.

Read-Only Operations: For most tasks, I have read-only access to client data. I can read matter information, client details, and document metadata—but cannot modify or delete existing client data.

Write-Only for Generated Output: When I generate output (engagement letters, invoices, time entries), I write only the newly generated document back to your system. I never modify historical client records.

Temporal Memory Isolation: Each session runs in isolated memory that is discarded after session completion. There is no persistent storage of client data in my systems. No data carries over between sessions.

Section 4: Audit Trail Verification

Every action I take is logged in your practice management system's audit trail, not in my systems. This means you control the audit trail—not me. You can view exactly what data was accessed when, by whom, and for what purpose.

These immutable logs satisfy ABA requirements to maintain "reasonable efforts to prevent unauthorized access" and to maintain records of data access for privilege protection. If privilege is ever challenged, you have irrefutable documentation of safeguards.

Healthcare AI systems use FHIR (Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources) standards for protecting sensitive data. Legal AI orchestration uses equivalent REST API standards with comparable protections. The architecture is proven and industry-standard for sensitive information.

Business Associate Agreements in healthcare require providers to implement exactly this type of ephemeral access for protected health information. I operate under similar BAA-equivalent agreements for legal tech, with the same stringent data protection requirements.

Section 6: Regulatory Compliance Implications

State bar opinions increasingly require lawyers to understand how AI vendors handle data. My architecture allows you to truthfully represent that:

  • Client data is never stored in my systems
  • Client data is never used for training or product improvement
  • Client data is accessed only for specific contracted tasks
  • All access is logged with complete audit trails
  • Access tokens expire after each session
  • No persistent data retention occurs

This directly satisfies state bar requirements for technology competence, vendor management, and confidentiality protection.
