ROI Calculator: AI vs Paralegal Costs for Law Firms

Law firms face a labor crisis: experienced paralegals are retiring, younger talent demands remote flexibility and higher pay, and turnover rates exceed 25% annually. Meanwhile, administrative workload keeps increasing—more compliance requirements, more complex billing, more client communication demands.

The question isn't whether to use AI. It's whether you can afford not to.

The Full Cost of a Paralegal

Law firms typically budget only the base salary when evaluating paralegal costs. But the true cost of a full-time employee extends far beyond the paycheck. Let me break down the complete financial picture—including the hidden costs that often surprise firm management when they analyze profitability.

Base Salary

$55,000-$75,000 depending on experience and market (NALA 2024 survey)

Entry-level paralegals with 0-2 years experience typically start at $45,000-$55,000 in smaller markets, while experienced paralegals (5+ years) in major metropolitan areas command $65,000-$85,000. Specialized paralegals (litigation support, e-discovery, intellectual property) can exceed $90,000 in competitive markets like New York, San Francisco, or Washington DC.

But salary is just the beginning. The fully-loaded cost of an employee includes mandatory taxes, benefits, overhead, and hidden productivity costs.

Benefits & Payroll Taxes

These are the legally required and competitive-market benefits that firms must provide:

  • Employer FICA (Social Security + Medicare): 7.65% = $4,200-$5,750 annually. This is mandatory federal payroll tax that employers pay on top of wages.
  • Health insurance (employer portion): $8,500-$12,000/year. The average employer contribution for single coverage is $7,700 (Kaiser Family Foundation 2024), but family coverage—which many paralegals elect—pushes employer costs to $15,000-$18,000 annually.
  • 401(k) match (3% typical): $1,650-$2,250. To remain competitive, most firms match employee 401(k) contributions up to 3-5% of salary.
  • Paid time off (15 days average): $3,200-$4,300. This is the cost of salary paid for days not worked. Paralegals typically receive 10-15 vacation days plus 5-10 sick days annually.
  • Workers' compensation insurance: $800-$1,200. Required in all states, rates vary by state and firm size. Legal services typically pay $0.50-$1.50 per $100 of payroll.
  • State unemployment tax (SUTA): $400-$800. Varies by state; California charges significantly more than Texas or Florida.
  • Disability insurance: $300-$600. Some states (CA, NY, NJ, RI, HI) mandate short-term disability insurance; many firms provide it voluntarily.

Total Benefits & Taxes: $19,050-$23,400 (approximately 30-35% of base salary)

Overhead Costs

Every employee requires physical space, equipment, and support resources:

  • Office space: $3,000-$6,000/year. Commercial office space in legal districts costs $30-$60 per square foot annually. A paralegal workspace (100-150 sq ft including shared common areas) translates to $3,000-$9,000/year depending on market. Firms in Manhattan or downtown San Francisco pay significantly more; suburban or secondary markets pay less.
  • Equipment (computer, software licenses): $2,000-$3,000/year. Laptop or desktop ($1,200-$1,800 amortized over 3 years), dual monitors ($400), Microsoft Office 365 ($150/year), practice management software seat license ($600-$1,200/year), legal research access (Westlaw/LexisNexis seat: $300-$500/year), document management system ($200-$400/year).
  • IT support & cybersecurity: $600-$1,200/year. Managed IT services, email hosting, data backup, cybersecurity insurance, VPN access for remote work.
  • Training & continuing education: $1,500-$2,500/year. CLE courses for paralegals (12-15 hours annually required in many states), software training, practice area specialization courses.
  • Supplies & miscellaneous: $500-$800/year. Office supplies, printing costs, business cards, professional association dues (NALA, state paralegal associations).

Total Overhead: $7,600-$13,500

Turnover Costs (amortized annually at 25% rate)

Paralegal turnover averages 25-30% annually in private law firms (NALA Utilization Survey 2024). This means one in four paralegals leaves each year—requiring recruitment, training, and dealing with lost productivity during vacancy periods. Even if your specific paralegal doesn't leave this year, you're statistically likely to experience turnover across your paralegal staff, and these costs should be amortized into your annual budget:

  • Recruitment costs: $2,000-$3,000 per hire. Job board postings ($400-$800), recruiter fees if used (15-25% of first-year salary, but amortized across multiple hires), staff time conducting interviews (15-20 hours at $50-$75/hour = $750-$1,500), background checks and drug screening ($150-$300).
  • Training time (3 months at 50% productivity): $6,900-$9,400. New paralegals require 60-90 days to reach full productivity. During this period, they're learning your practice management software, document templates, client communication protocols, and firm-specific procedures. At 50% productivity, you're paying full salary ($13,750-$18,750 for 3 months) but receiving half the output—effective loss of $6,875-$9,375.
  • Lost productivity during vacancy (2-4 weeks): $2,100-$5,800. Time between paralegal departure and new hire start date. Work either doesn't get done (delaying cases), gets redistributed to other staff (reducing their productivity), or gets done by higher-cost attorneys (reducing profitability).
  • Severance & unemployment: $1,000-$2,000. If termination is involuntary, severance packages (1-4 weeks pay) and increased unemployment insurance rates.

Turnover Costs: $12,000-$20,200 per departure
Amortized annually at 25% turnover rate: $3,000-$5,050

Total Annual Cost of One Paralegal

Adding it all together:

  • Base salary: $55,000-$75,000
  • Benefits & taxes: $19,050-$23,400
  • Overhead: $7,600-$13,500
  • Amortized turnover: $3,000-$5,050

Total: $84,650-$116,950 per paralegal

For budgeting purposes, law firms should estimate $90,000-$120,000 fully-loaded cost per paralegal FTE. This is 1.5-1.7x base salary—a multiple that catches many small firm owners by surprise when they first calculate true employment costs.

Billable Hour Capture: The Hidden ROI

Direct labor cost savings are just the beginning. The larger financial impact comes from recovering billable hours that are currently leaking out of your firm through inadequate time tracking, delayed billing, and write-downs. Let me break down how AI-powered automation transforms revenue realization.

Time Leakage Reduction

Law firms lose 10-15% of billable time due to inadequate time capture (Clio Legal Trends Report 2024). Attorneys forget to log time entries, underestimate hours worked, or write off time because they can't remember what they did three days ago when they finally get around to entering time.

Current state: 10% time leakage on 15,000 billable hour target = 1,500 unbilled hours
Average billing rate: $300/hour (blended rate across partners, senior associates, junior associates)
Annual revenue loss: 1,500 hours × $300 = $450,000

I reduce time leakage from 10% to 2% through automated time capture:

  • Email time tracking: When you send an email related to a client matter, I automatically create a draft time entry: "0.2 hours, Email to opposing counsel re: discovery extension, Matter XYZ." You review and approve in one click.
  • Document work tracking: When you work on a document in your DMS (iManage, NetDocuments), I track time spent and create entries: "1.3 hours, Draft motion for summary judgment, Matter ABC."
  • Calendar-based entries: After a client meeting or court appearance on your calendar, I prompt: "Log time for Smith deposition (3 hours scheduled). Actual time spent?" You confirm or adjust.

With Claire: 2% time leakage = 300 unbilled hours
Recovered billable time: 1,200 hours × $300 = $360,000/year

Faster Billing Cycles & Cash Flow

The faster you bill, the faster you get paid. Manual invoice generation introduces delays:

  • End of month: Paralegal pulls unbilled time entries from practice management system
  • Days 1-3: Reviewing attorney verifies time entries, identifies write-downs
  • Days 4-7: Paralegal generates invoices, attorney reviews for accuracy
  • Days 8-10: Invoices mailed to clients
  • Day 40: Average payment received (30 days from invoice date)

Current state: 40 days from work performed to payment received

I automate invoice generation and reduce billing lag from 30 days to 7 days:

  • End of month: I automatically generate invoices using your firm template
  • Day 1: Attorney reviews and approves (no manual data entry required)
  • Day 1: Invoices automatically sent via email to clients (or uploaded to client portal)
  • Day 31: Payment received (30 days from invoice date)

With Claire: 31 days from work performed to payment received

Cash flow impact: 9 days faster billing × $200,000 average monthly revenue = $60,000 additional cash on hand (equivalent to a working capital improvement)

Higher Realization Rates

Realization rate = (Amount collected ÷ Amount billed). Industry average: 85-90%, meaning firms write off or fail to collect 10-15% of billed time.

Common reasons for write-downs:

  • Inaccurate time entries: "10 hours to draft simple complaint" gets written down to 6 hours because client disputes excessive time
  • Block billing: "Legal research and draft memorandum: 8.5 hours" is vague and invites scrutiny
  • Inconsistent billing practices: Junior associate billed at partner rate by mistake
  • Delayed billing: Invoice sent 90 days after work performed, client has forgotten the value delivered

I improve realization rates through:

  • Accurate time capture: Real-time tracking prevents overestimation
  • Detailed descriptions: I generate specific, defensible time entry descriptions ("Review opposing counsel's motion for summary judgment (23 pages), identify three procedural defects, research case law on timeliness objections: 2.3 hours")
  • Rate enforcement: I automatically apply correct billing rates based on who performed work (partner vs associate vs paralegal)
  • Write-down prevention: I flag time entries that exceed reasonable expectations before invoicing, allowing attorneys to revise proactively

Current realization rate: 87%
With Claire: 92% (5-point improvement)
On $2M in billings: 5% improvement = $100,000 additional collected revenue
